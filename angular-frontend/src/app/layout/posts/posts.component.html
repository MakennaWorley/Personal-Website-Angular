I didn’t start this semester expecting anything dramatic or crazy. I thought I’d finish my classes, keep working my internship, and slowly chip away at my Computer Science/Data Science capstone — the kind of slow, steady grind that feels safe. Something that I've done for the last 6 semesters.

But somewhere between drafting goals for Assignment 1 and interviewing Jesse for Assignment 3, something shifted. I started seeing myself differently. Not just as a student juggling three different college "tracks", not just as someone who always raises the bar with each assignment, but as an engineer in the making.

It really began when I wrote out my semester goals — the simple question of whether I was leaning toward software engineering or data science. The answer, I thought, would come from coursework or a career quiz. Instead, it came from building something almost absurdly ambitious: my own database system in Rust.

What started as a capstone idea turned into the first time I saw myself solving a truly foundational problem. I loved the puzzle of it — thinking about data storage, indexing, failure modes, the parts no one ever sees but everything depends on. It was the first hint that maybe my future sits at the intersection of systems, data, and applied problem-solving.

It was also the moment I realized:

I like hard problems.

I chase them, even when they scare me a little.

Researching Carnegie Mellon’s PhD program should have clarified whether I wanted to go straight into grad school. It didn’t. Instead, it revealed that what I actually want is challenge with support, not challenge for its own sake. I learned that I’m excited by research and big ideas — but I’m not ready to disappear into academia just yet. I want time in industry. I want to understand real systems before I disappear into theoretical ones.

And then came the interview with Jesse — the moment everything quietly clicked.

I expected job-market stories or warnings about burnout. Instead, he described something no class had put into words: the difference between writing code and engineering. The first makes things work. The second makes things that last.

He talked about how his team measured progress, how stability mattered more than constant output, how real engineering often means testing and reviewing more than writing new features. I remember sitting there, thinking:

This is what I want. This is how I want to build.

Not fast — but thoughtfully.

Not alone — but collaboratively.

Not perfectly — but intentionally.

It suddenly made sense why my internship at Ken Garff feels like the right place to grow. I’ve already seen the difference between a company that values people and one that values output. I’ve experienced the work culture that treats interns like rising engineers, not temporary labor. I’ve watched how mentorship, curiosity, and trust turn tech work into something sustainable.

By the time I finished the final assignment, I noticed something surprising:
My plans didn’t radically change.
I did.

This semester taught me that my path doesn’t have to be a straight line — it can be iterative, like the systems I love to build. I can start in industry, learn the architecture of real systems, and later return to school if I’m still hungry for theory. I can be a full-stack engineer who thinks like a data scientist. I can build tools that support real people, not just abstract problems.

I’m not trying to “pick a future” anymore.
I’m designing one.

And for the first time, it feels like the story is starting to write itself.
