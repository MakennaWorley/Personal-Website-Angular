The Beginning

When I first arrived at Westminster, I had never taken a real computer science class. I had tinkered with Scratch, but stepping into CMPT 201 was like being dropped into cold water. I struggled through concepts I now have memorized to the point that I could repeat them in my sleep, and more than once I wondered if I had made a mistake majoring in Computer Science. But somewhere between debugging loops and convincing myself I wasn’t failing, I won the class UNO tournament — something small, but symbolic. I walked in terrified of finishing last, and I walked out winning. That pattern would repeat itself many times over the next seven semesters: I enter unsure, stubbornly try anyway, and end up redefining what I thought I could do.

By the end of my first semester, I had declared not just a Computer Science major, but also Mathematics, and joined the Honors College. I certainly hadn’t planned on any of that when I was wandering campus looking for HWAC with my nose glued to Google Maps. I just kept chasing the next challenge:

Calculus III? Sure.

Linear Algebra? Why not.

Research? Of course! Let’s do three projects: fall 2023, spring 2024, summer 2024.

I became someone who always had a side-project compiling, an idea simmering, or a new concept open in twenty tabs. I didn’t always feel confident, but I always felt determined. And determination can get you anywhere.

The Shift

That determination pushed me forward until semester six — the hardest semester of my entire degree. I took Abstract Algebra, AI, Differential Equations, and Operating Systems simultaneously, and decided to compete in my first hackathon because a professor said it would be “good experience.” My team looked at each other and said, “Sure, why not,” which is also a theme of my academic life.

Then Abstract Algebra hit me harder than anything I’d taken before. I cried over homework multiple times — something even Topology hadn’t managed — and I felt myself losing the joy I once had for math. Dropping the Math major with only two classes left was one of the hardest decisions I’ve made. It felt like letting go of a version of myself I had worked incredibly hard to build. But it also taught me something important: growth isn’t only about pushing harder. Sometimes it’s about stepping back before you break.

Letting go opened space for something new. I had already taken a few Data Science classes, and it turned out I could finish the entire minor in my last year. I might be the most chaotic Data Science student Westminster has ever had — taking Bayesian Data Analysis before Modern Stats, learning R in a day, and begging the department chair to let me into Statistical Learning before Statistical Modeling. Somehow he said yes, and somehow I fell in love with the field. My path has never been linear, but it has always moved me closer to the work that excites me.

The Plan for What's Next

Now I’m at the end of my college journey: a Computer Science major with minors in Data Science and Mathematics, an Honors student, and a First-Gen student. But the question remains — what now?

Data science or computer science?

People assume they’re the same, but they’re not — and the assignments I completed this semester helped me see how differently I engage with each.

I tried to choose by making pros and cons lists, researching job paths, and reflecting on my assignments and discussions from my college experience. Instead, I learned something unexpected: I’m not supposed to have certainty yet — and that’s okay. My internship shows I already live in the overlap. I build dashboards in Domo, write Angular/NestJS applications, optimize Snowflake schemas, and design research projects blending computation and modeling. Even my capstone is a hybrid: exploring whether machine learning can reconstruct ancestral genetic data.

So my plan is this:

Work full-time as a software engineer (ideally at Ken Garff, but I’m open).

Continue growing in data science through projects, certifications, and curiosity.

Revisit graduate school after gaining real industry experience — or maybe not.

This plan matches the version of myself I’ve finally gotten to know: someone who learns by doing, thrives in collaborative environments, and doesn’t need to choose a single lane right this second.

Right now my next steps are simple:

Finish my presentations.

Keep building my Rust database project.

Keep learning, keep making things, keep saying “I can learn that.”

Keep designing my life around curiosity, challenge, and community.

If I strip everything down, here’s what remains true:

I like hard problems.

I like making things — not fast, but thoughtfully.

Not perfectly — but intentionally.

I don’t have all the answers for my future yet, but I finally understand that I don’t need to.

I know how to work, how to learn, and how to rebuild myself when something breaks.

And for where I’m heading next —

that is enough.
